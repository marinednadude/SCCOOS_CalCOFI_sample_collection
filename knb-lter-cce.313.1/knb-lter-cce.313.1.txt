    2022-07-11These euphausiid data are generated from samples taken by the CalCOFI (California Cooperative Oceanic Fisheries Investigations) program, starting with the 1951 cruises.  Data are obtained by enumeration of samples by trained microscopists with a high level of taxonomic expertise, beginning with the late Edward Brinton, including Annie Townsend’s assistance starting in the 1970’s, and then by Linsey Sala from 2011 to present.  Only a limited subset of the CalCOFI samples have been enumerated taxonomically, primarily from springtime cruises. Since 2004, taxonomic analysis has been carried out as part of the CCE-LTER (California Current Ecosystem Long-Term Ecological Research) program, supported by the U.S. National Science Foundation.  The primary geographic area analyzed is the Southern California region (CalCOFI lines 77 to 93, inclusive), supplemented by more limited springtime cruises from the Central California region (lines 60 to 73).  Samples are all double oblique samples taken with a bongo net beginning in Dec. 1977 or a ring net prior to that time, nominally from 210-0 m, bottom depth permitting.

        The user should be aware that both the geographic coverage and temporal frequency of CalCOFI cruises have changed since the program was initiated in 1949.  Extreme care should be taken to evaluate these changes prior to generating time series of euphausiid abundance (see: https://calcofi.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=115&Itemid=520).

Also, the maximum zooplankton sampling depth in the CalCOFI time series increased from 0-70 m (1949-1950) to 0-140 m in 1951, was then maintained at 0-140 m from 1951-1968, then increased to 0-210 m in 1969, where it has remained since (see Ohman and Smith 1995).  A 1-m diameter ring net (0.55 mm mesh) was used from 1949-1976, which was then replaced with a 0.71-m diameter bongo net (0.505 mm mesh) in December 1977 (Ohman and Smith 1995).  Effects of these changes have been analyzed by Brinton and Townsend (1981), Ohman and Smith (1995), and Ohman and Lavaniegos (2002).

Net deployment protocols may be found at https://cce.lternet.edu/data/methods-manual/augmented-cruises/calbobl-net-depolyment.  Currently, a 0.71-m diameter, 0.505-mm mesh bongo net (McGowan and Brown 1966) is lowered to 300 m wire out (nominally to a depth of 210 m, with a 45° wire angle) with the ship underway at 0.5-1.0 m s-1, held at depth for 30 seconds, then retrieved to the surface at a constant rate (20 m min-1).  The starboard side sample analyzed here is preserved in a 1.8% final solution of formaldehyde in seawater, buffered with a saturated solution of sodium tetraborate.  Flow meters are calibrated regularly.  Samples are curated at the Pelagic Invertebrate Collection, Scripps Institution of Oceanography, until analysis. 

Only a subset of CalCOFI samples have been analyzed for euphausiids. Recently, M. Ohman's retrospective studies have focused on interannual variability in springtime cruises, hence more up-to-date temporal coverage is available for springtime cruises than for cruises at other times of year (see Coverage Table at: http://oceaninformatics-dev.ucsd.edu/euphausiid/docs/BTEDBCoverage_20210216.pdf).

The same group of 24 euphausiid species has been identified in all samples analyzed, using consistent morphological criteria. However, the level of resolution of life history phases or size classes has varied over time, according to the goals of the original study. The enumerations in this database were not originally conceived as a continuous time series, but represent a compendium of enumerations from many individual research projects. A table indicating the level of life history or size class resolution for specific cruises may be found in the Coverage Table. For cruises in which samples were identified only by size, the corresponding life history phases have been approximated using the information found in the Size-to-Phase Table.  Most of the samples analyzed are only nighttime samples, to avoid artifacts induced by daytime net avoidance and by Diel Vertical Migration of euphausiids.

Laboratory analysis entails removing appropriate aliquots with a Folsom splitter or Stempel pipette, conducting enumerations under a dissecting microscope to species and either life history phase or size class, then correcting data for appropriate aliquoting factors and volumes of seawater filtered in situ.  The data are expressed as the vertically integrated number of individuals beneath 1 m2 of sea surface, not as volumetric density (number of individuals per m3).   This is done because the maximum sampling depth was less than 210 m in shallow waters, which can generate a bias if volumetric densities are used.  No. m-2 is obtained by multiplying the No. m-3 by the maximum sampling depth. The code for missing values is N/A.

      Brinton and Townsend Euphausiid Abundance Data
      Euphausiid data from the California Current System, based upon over 10,000 zooplankton samples from more than 200 cruises, spanning 1951 to the present. In total, 39 species of euphausiids have been identified and enumerated in this region.  Each sample was screened for the same set of species, and zero values represent an absence of a given taxon in the sample.
